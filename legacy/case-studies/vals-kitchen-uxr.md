# Val's Kitchen Software — UX Research

**Summary:** Co-researcher on a group that designed and usability-tested a restaurant kitchen inventory app from a real client's inventory sheets through Figma prototype. COGS 187A, [CONFIRM: quarter/year].

---

## Context

Val's Kitchen had no existing digital inventory system — staff were working from physical sheets. The design challenge was to create an app that let kitchen workers quickly locate items and check their stock and freshness status during active service. The environment is high-pressure and fast-moving, which made speed and scannability the primary design constraints.

## My Role

Contributed to user testing design, moderation, and synthesis. I helped write the testing scenarios, moderated sessions, observed and recorded participant behavior, and applied heuristic analysis to the results.

## Approach

- **Scenario-based testing design:** Rather than abstract tasks, we wrote four role-grounded scenarios that reflected real workflows in the kitchen: opening shift lead (learning the color key), cook (locating specific ingredients quickly), night/closing shift lead (identifying items that need restocking), and inventory manager (full stock audit). Each scenario was designed to stress-test a different part of the interface.
- **4 users, 4 scenarios:** Each participant was assigned a unique scenario so we covered the range of user types. Each team member moderated one session, using a consistent protocol: orientation to the Google Sheets context, task execution, and observation of errors and successes.
- **Heuristic tagging:** After sessions, we coded findings against Nielsen's heuristics. Successes mapped to hierarchy, match between system and real world, and minimalist design. Problems mapped to flexibility and efficiency of use, visibility of system status, and cognitive load.
- **Synthesis into a findings table:** Organized results into "what users liked" vs. "pain points" categories, which gave the team a shared, actionable reference for the redesign sprint.

## Outcome

Key usability findings that drove the hi-fi redesign: users praised clear hierarchy and minimalist layout but reported scroll fatigue from the flat item list, confusion from the color-coded location labels, and icon sizes that were too small for quick scanning. One participant doing the cook scenario noted the interface was hard to read because too much information appeared at once on the home screen. These findings directly justified splitting the home screen into a category-first view and adding an inline low-stock alert system.

## Reflection

With more time I'd have recruited actual restaurant staff rather than proxies. The scenarios were grounded in real kitchen roles, but testers who had never worked a kitchen rush hour couldn't fully simulate the time pressure that would surface the real usability failures.
