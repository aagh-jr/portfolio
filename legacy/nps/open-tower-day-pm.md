## Context

Open Tower Day is a one-day public event at Cabrillo National Monument. The park needed materials that would guide visitors, encourage engagement with specific park features, and work for a wide age range — all within the physical and logistical constraints of a real outdoor event. There's no second print run once the event starts.

## My Role

[CONFIRM: sole designer? Part of a team?] Responsible for scoping the materials, selecting activities, and delivering print-ready files ahead of the event date.

## Approach

- **Scope decisions under hard constraints:** The materials had to be printable, distributable, and self-explanatory — no digital dependencies, no staff required to explain them. That ruled out anything requiring an app, QR code, or ongoing facilitation.
- **Two-format product decision:** An activity booklet (1 page, all-ages, completion-incentive structure) and a Historical Explorer passport (multi-page, lighthouse-focused, older visitors) served different segments of the visitor population. Separating them meant neither was watered down to work for everyone.
- **Activity prioritization:** Five activity types made it in — word hunt, drawing prompt, stamp collection, staff interview, and bingo with park-specific items (Sea Dahlia, Shaw's Agave, CA Towhee, Cabrillo Statue). Each activity corresponds to a real location or interaction at the park; none is just a fill-in-the-blank worksheet.
- **Prize incentive as completion mechanic:** Completing the booklet enters visitors in a prize drawing. This is a PM-level decision about what motivates the target audience (kids) to complete all activities rather than stopping after the first one.
- **Print deadline as forcing function:** Event date meant a hard ship date for files. Scope was locked early; the focus was execution quality.

## Reflection

Designing for a fixed event with a firm deadline and real visitors is a completely different muscle from iterative digital work. There's no "push an update" after the event. The pressure of that finality sharpens your prioritization instincts quickly.
