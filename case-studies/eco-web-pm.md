# Eco-Web Educational Card Game — Product Management

**Summary:** Produced a two-set ecological card game that teaches California food web concepts through species identification and disruption scenario reasoning. [CONFIRM: class project / NPS internship at Cabrillo National Monument, timeframe].

## Context

Environmental education materials for public visitors and students often default to posters, pamphlets, or slide decks — formats that don't invite active participation. The challenge was creating something that could be used in a structured educational activity and that accurately represented the specific ecosystems of [CONFIRM: Cabrillo NM / Southern California].

## My Role

[CONFIRM: solo or team project? I'll assume solo or primary designer/producer unless confirmed otherwise.] I scoped the content, made decisions about what species to include, designed the two card formats, and produced the final printable files.

## Approach

- **Scope: two ecosystems, two card types:** The product scope was two parallel but distinct ecological systems (coastal scrub / inland + intertidal / marine) each with its own species set. The scenario cards cut across both ecosystems with disruption events relevant to each. This gave facilitators flexibility to use one ecosystem or both depending on session length.
- **Content selection tradeoffs:** Deciding which species to include required balancing representativeness (covering multiple trophic levels), recognizability (species a student might actually encounter), and scientific accuracy. The final selection includes producers, primary consumers, secondary consumers, and apex predators for both ecosystems.
- **Scenario design for reasoning, not recall:** The PM decision here was to make the scenarios open-ended prompts ("What do you think will happen?") rather than answer cards. This makes the game facilitator-led and discussion-based, which fits a classroom or guided NPS context better than a self-directed quiz format.
- **Print-ready format:** The double-sided card format (Animal Cards) and single-sided format (Scenario Cards) were chosen to fit standard printing constraints while keeping the visual design readable at card scale.

## Outcome

Two complete printable card sets, ready for educational use. [CONFIRM: were these actually printed and used in a session? Feedback from educators or participants?]

## Reflection

The open-ended scenario card design was the right call, but it places a lot of weight on the facilitator. A future version would include a facilitator guide with example discussion prompts and correct answer frameworks — not to script the session, but to make the product usable without prior ecological knowledge.
