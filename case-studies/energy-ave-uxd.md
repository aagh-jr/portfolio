# Energy Avenue — UX Design

**Summary:** Designer on an 8-person team that built a gamified energy conservation experience for PlanetFlip — from sketch to fully playable in-browser game. ENG 100D, Winter 2025, 10 weeks.

---

## Context

PlanetFlip needed a game that could teach energy conservation to younger users without feeling like a lesson. Existing educational games either leaned too heavily on text or lacked the visual feedback loops that make games feel rewarding. The design challenge was building something that stayed playable and immersive while embedding real educational content throughout.

## My Role

Designer and researcher on the team. I worked across ideation, sketching, and the visual design of game levels, and contributed to the transition from lo-fi wireframes to hi-fi screens.

## Approach

- **Competitive reference:** Before sketching, I reviewed existing games for what worked visually and mechanically — PBS Kids' Space Waste Odyssey for environmental content framing, and several gamified learning tools for points and feedback patterns. The goal was to understand the conventions so we could make deliberate choices rather than reinvent poorly.
- **Level structure:** Chose to organize the game by room (Bedroom, Bathroom, Kitchen) rather than by topic or difficulty. Rooms are concrete and familiar to the target audience; players can apply what they learn to their actual home immediately.
- **Lo-fi → hi-fi iteration:** Early wireframes were validated in Round 1 user testing, which revealed that affordances weren't obvious enough — users couldn't always tell what was interactive. We added clearer visual signifiers in the hi-fi pass.
- **Feedback and reward design:** Points, animations, and an energy meter worked together to give players immediate feedback on their choices. The in-game feedback cues (not just a score screen at the end) kept players oriented and motivated during gameplay.
- **Visual tone:** Vibrant colors, simple character-friendly environments, and in-context educational info — displayed as part of the game world rather than as a tooltip or modal — kept the aesthetic cohesive and age-appropriate.

## Outcome

A fully playable three-level in-browser game delivered at the end of the 10-week course. Round 2 user testers responded positively to the animation feedback and the atmosphere: *"I found the atmosphere very nice, I appreciated the in-game feedback, very immersive."* [CONFIRM: whether screenshots or a live link to the game are shareable for portfolio display]

## Reflection

The strongest design decision we made was keeping interactions click-based and simple — no complex input schemes. It made the game accessible and kept the engineering scope manageable. I'd want to push more on visual accessibility (contrast, color-blind modes) in a future version; we prioritized vibrancy but didn't fully audit accessibility.
