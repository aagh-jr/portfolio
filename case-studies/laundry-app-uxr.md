# UCSD Laundry App — UX Research

**Summary:** Researcher on a group design project to redesign the UCSD communal laundry experience, conducting user interviews and synthesizing findings into a prototype. COMM 124A (Participatory Design), [CONFIRM: quarter/year].

## Context

UCSD's on-campus laundry rooms are a shared resource with visibility, accountability, and lost-and-found problems that affect students daily. COMM 124A framed design as a participatory, community-centered practice — the research goal wasn't just to find usability problems but to understand what students actually needed from a system that shapes a part of their living environment.

## My Role

Researcher on the team. I contributed to the interview guide design, participated in user interviews, and helped analyze research notes into design insights. [CONFIRM: whether I recruited participants or co-moderated sessions]

## Approach

- **Interview guide development:** The team designed structured interview questions to understand current laundry behaviors, frustrations, and workarounds. Topics included machine availability, lost-and-found experiences, and what information students would need to make a laundry trip worthwhile. [CONFIRM: number of interviews conducted]
- **Observational component:** The team produced a "Bird's Eye View" spatial brainstorm of the laundry room, suggesting observation of the physical space in addition to interviews — mapping how people actually move through and use the environment.
- **Affinity mapping and brainstorming:** Research notes and interview responses were grouped into themes. The brainstorming session identified features to address: machine status visibility, lost-and-found reporting, and in-app notifications.
- **Mood board:** Created to capture the tone and user expectations for a laundry app — practical, low-friction, trustworthy. Not a consumer app, a campus utility.
- **Participatory framing:** Prof. Irani's course framed design as a political and community act, which pushed the team to consider whose interests a redesign would serve — and whether a better app actually addressed the root problem or just smoothed it over.

## Outcome

Research fed directly into lo-fi and hi-fi Figma prototypes. Key features surfaced by research: machine availability display, lost-and-found reporting flow, and a notification/alert system. [CONFIRM: whether the prototype or research was presented to UCSD Housing or HDCS]

## Reflection

The participatory design lens from this course changed how I think about research. It's easy to frame a laundry problem as "users need better information" — but the better question is why the information gap exists in the first place and who benefits from leaving it unaddressed. That framing produces different — and better — design questions.
