## Context

UCSD on-campus laundry rooms have no real-time status visibility, no lost-and-found system, and no way to report problems. Students work around this with timing guesses and informal communication. The challenge was scoping a product that addressed real student needs — not just the most technically tractable features.

## My Role

Contributor to scoping, feature prioritization, and team coordination. I helped identify which problems the app should solve and which it shouldn't, based on what research surfaced. [CONFIRM: whether I owned any specific feature or sprint]

## Approach

- **Problem framing from research:** The team resisted jumping straight to "build a machine-status app." User interviews and spatial observation revealed that lost-and-found and accountability problems were equally painful — and less addressed by existing tools. The scope expanded accordingly.
- **Feature prioritization:** Three feature areas made it into the prototype: machine availability display, lost-and-found reporting, and in-app problem reporting. Features like laundry scheduling and payment integration were noted but deferred — too complex for the course timeline and not validated by research.
- **Participatory scope check:** COMM 124A's political design framing pushed the team to ask: who is this for, and who decides? The research phase included community members, not just individual users. That affected what we treated as requirements.
- **Mood board as alignment tool:** Before jumping into wireframes, the team built a mood board to align on tone — campus utility, not consumer app. This prevented the design from drifting toward something more polished and impractical than what students actually needed.
- **Lo-fi before hi-fi discipline:** The team committed to a functional lo-fi before moving to hi-fi. This kept the feature set honest — it's easy to add polish to a concept that doesn't yet work.

Lo-fi and hi-fi Figma prototypes covering the machine availability, lost-and-found, and reporting flows. [CONFIRM: total number of screens in final hi-fi; whether prototype was presented to campus stakeholders]

## Reflection

The course's political design framing is something I'd carry into any product work. The instinct to ask "who does this serve, and at whose expense" isn't just an academic exercise — it surfaces requirements that a pure usability frame misses entirely.
