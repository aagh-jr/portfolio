# Energy Avenue — UX Research

**Summary:** Researcher on an 8-person team that designed and user-tested a browser-based sustainability game for PlanetFlip. ENG 100D, Winter 2025, 10 weeks.

---

## Context

PlanetFlip partners with climate organizations to create gamified platforms that drive community action. Their existing tools lacked the kind of interactive, engaging experience that would actually change behavior in younger users. The challenge was figuring out what game mechanics and presentation styles would make energy conservation feel relevant and fun to adolescents, not like a classroom assignment.

## My Role

Designer and researcher on the team. I contributed to competitive analysis, helped shape the user testing methodology for both rounds, moderated and observed sessions, and synthesized findings into redesign direction.

## Approach

- **Competitive audit:** Analyzed existing games (Financial Freedom by Legends of Learning, Space Waste Odyssey by PBS Kids, Fidget Power, NEF's Energy Champion) to extract what made them engaging — points systems, energy meters, cohesive storylines, real-world relevance.
- **Scoping the research question:** Focused on two questions: what mechanics reinforce learning without feeling didactic, and what difficulty level works for a K-12 audience that may have no prior context on energy conservation.
- **Round 1 testing (wireframes):** Showed participants individual frames from the lo-fi prototype and asked them to identify every element they believed they could interact with. Tracked what they missed and what drew their attention first.
- **Round 2 testing (gameplay + survey):** Had participants play the hi-fi prototype, then collected responses via a 7-question survey. Combined observational notes with direct quotes to capture both behavior and perception.
- **Synthesis:** Grouped findings into what worked (point animations reinforcing positive loops, immersive room environments, in-game feedback cues) and what didn't (text-heavy tutorials that broke flow, unclear affordances in early wireframes).

## Outcome

Two rounds of user testing produced actionable design changes that made it into the final hi-fi. Positive quotes from participants: *"After you get points the animations made it feel very rewarding"* and *"I found the atmosphere very nice, I appreciated the in-game feedback, very immersive."* The final product was a fully playable in-browser game with three levels (Bedroom, Bathroom, Kitchen), built and delivered within the 10-week course.

## Reflection

I'd push harder next time to recruit participants closer to the actual target age range — most of our testers were college-age, which likely smoothed over some usability friction a younger player would hit. Controlling for that earlier would have made the findings sharper.
