# Open Tower Day — UX Design

**Summary:** Designed and produced visitor engagement materials — an activity booklet and a "Historical Explorer" passport — for a live public event at Cabrillo National Monument. [CONFIRM: internship / volunteer / class context, timeframe].

## Context

Cabrillo National Monument's Open Tower Day brings the public into the Old Point Loma Lighthouse for a special event. The park needed materials that would guide visitors through specific locations and activities, engage a wide age range, and feel worth keeping — not like a throwaway worksheet. Everything had to work in print, in a hands-on outdoor environment.

## My Role

[CONFIRM: solo or team?] Designed both printed deliverables — the single-page activity booklet and the multi-page Historical Explorer passport — from concept through print-ready files.

## Approach

- **Activity booklet: completion-arc structure:** The booklet is structured around a completion reward ("Complete this booklet for a chance to win a gift!"). This gives it a beginning, middle, and end — visitors aren't just wandering, they're on a mission. Activities include: a word hunt, a drawing prompt (the Old Point Loma Lighthouse), a stamp collection at both lighthouse locations, a staff interview activity, event participation tracking, and a bingo card with park-specific items.
- **Bingo as wayfinding:** The bingo card (Sea Dahlia, Shaw's Agave, CA Towhee, Cabrillo Statue) is also a scavenger hunt. It encourages visitors to look for specific plants and landmarks they'd otherwise walk past. The game structure does the interpretive work without a sign.
- **Historical Explorer passport:** The multi-page passport format creates a collectible artifact — something a visitor takes home and might actually keep. The "LIGHTHOUSE EXPLORER" and "HISTORICAL EXPLORER" sections are stamped or filled as visitors move through the park, mirroring the format of real national park passports.
- **Typography and illustration:** Designed for print legibility at small size, with illustrated headers and a format that reads clearly outdoors. [CONFIRM: what tools were used for design — Canva, Illustrator, etc.?]
- **Two-audience design:** The booklet is for all ages (kids primarily). The passport leans toward older visitors. Both use the same park-branded visual language, but the density and content depth differ.

## Outcome

Print-ready files for both deliverables, produced for a real public event at a National Monument. These are public-facing, real-world design artifacts — not a class prototype. [CONFIRM: can finished design files or photos of the printed materials be shared as portfolio samples?]

## Reflection

Designing for print in a real-world context forced constraints that digital work doesn't: legibility at small size, durability of the format, and no ability to iterate after distribution. I'd bring those constraints into digital work more deliberately — designs that hold up without tooltips, hover states, or error recovery are better designs.
