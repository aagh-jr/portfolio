## Context

Students and visitors often learn about ecosystems in the abstract, disconnected from the specific species and disruption events that happen in their local environment. A card game format offers hands-on engagement with food web logic — and grounds it in real California coastal and inland species.

## Approach

- **Audience and context:** The game was designed for [CONFIRM: age group? classroom use? NPS guided activity?]. Understanding the audience determined how much scientific vocabulary was included and how the prompts were phrased.
- **Two-ecosystem scope:** The decision to cover both inland coastal scrub (bumble bees, prickly pear, hawks, foxes, gopher snakes) and intertidal/marine (Garibaldi, dolphin, bat star, hermit crab, brown algae) reflected the geography of [CONFIRM: Cabrillo NM's two distinct habitats]. Each ecosystem gets its own card set.
- **Scenario card design:** Rather than testing recall, the scenario cards prompt reasoning: "A new predator species has been introduced. What happens to the food chain?" This design choice pushes users to apply their understanding rather than retrieve a fact.
- **Content accuracy:** All species include scientific names alongside common names. The food chain icons (Sunlight, Water, Nutrients) are consistent across both sets to establish shared vocabulary.

## Outcome

Two printed card sets: Animal Cards (double-sided, coastal and marine species) and Scenario Cards (disruption events including disease, drought, chemical spill, ocean acidification, and invasive species). ## Reflection

The scenario cards are where the real learning happens. If I were to push this further, I'd run a play session with the target audience and observe where reasoning breaks down — that would tell me which trophic relationships weren't communicated clearly enough by the card design.
