# Eco-Web Educational Card Game — UX Design

**Summary:** Designed and produced a two-set physical card game teaching California food web concepts — species cards for inland/coastal and marine ecosystems, plus scenario cards for disruption reasoning. [CONFIRM: class project / NPS internship at Cabrillo National Monument, timeframe].

---

## Context

Environmental education at places like [CONFIRM: Cabrillo National Monument] reaches visitors with a wide range of prior knowledge and attention spans. A card game format creates active participation rather than passive reading — and can scale from a quick visitor activity to a structured classroom session. The design challenge was making the card system intuitive enough to pick up in a few minutes while still communicating accurate ecological science.

## My Role

[CONFIRM: solo or team project?] I designed both card types — Animal Cards (double-sided) and Scenario Cards — including species selection, trophic hierarchy representation, icon system, and visual layout for print.

## Approach

- **Double-sided card format:** Animal Cards show the species name (common + scientific), its diet/prey icons (Mammals, Birds, Bugs, Fish, etc.), and the trophic inputs (Sunlight, Water, Nutrients) at the producer level. The double-sided format puts additional ecological context on the back without cluttering the front. This lets players reference a card quickly during play and dig deeper if curious.
- **Shared icon system:** A consistent visual vocabulary across both ecosystems — Sunlight, Water, Nutrient icons made of atomic diagrams (H₂O, CO₂, N/C chains) — communicates the producer-level inputs without text. This keeps the cards readable for younger users and creates visual coherence across 30+ cards.
- **Two ecosystem split:** Inland/coastal scrub (California Bumble Bee, Prickly Pear, Black Phoebe, Cooper's Hawk, Grey Fox, Gopher Snake, Red-tailed Hawk, Crawford's Grey Shrew, Desert Cottontail) and intertidal/marine (Brown Algae, Bat Star, Blueband Hermit Crab, Garibaldi, Osprey, Common Dolphin). Keeping them distinct lets facilitators use one set without needing the other, but the shared icon language lets them be combined for cross-ecosystem reasoning.
- **Scenario card design:** Six scenario types per ecosystem: disease, new predator, drought, chemical spill, invasive species, and an ecosystem-specific event (ocean acidification for marine; habitat takeover for inland). Each is written as a prompt — "What do you think will happen to the food chain?" — to drive discussion rather than test a single right answer.
- **Print-ready layout:** Designed for double-sided printing at standard card size. Species names, scientific names, and prey/input icons are all legible at card scale without microscopy.

## Reflection

The constraint of designing for print rather than a screen forced me to think carefully about information hierarchy at a much smaller scale. Every element on the card had to earn its place. That's a different design muscle than UI work, and it's one I'd like to exercise more.
