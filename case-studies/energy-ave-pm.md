# Energy Avenue — Product Management

**Summary:** Contributor on an 8-person cross-functional team that scoped, designed, tested, and shipped a browser-based sustainability game for a real client (PlanetFlip). ENG 100D, Winter 2025, 10 weeks.

---

## Context

PlanetFlip is a climate organization building gamified platforms for local sustainability groups. They needed a working educational game — not a prototype, a playable product — that a real organization could put in front of users. The team had 10 weeks, mixed skills (design, engineering, research), and a stakeholder ecosystem that included PlanetFlip, the National Park Service, and the San Diego Climate Collaborative.

## My Role

Designer and researcher, operating within a team that shared PM responsibilities. I contributed to prioritization decisions, helped coordinate across design and research workstreams, and was one of the primary contacts for translating user feedback into scoped design changes.

## Approach

- **Scoping the focus area:** The team decided early to anchor the game on energy conservation specifically — not climate change broadly. Energy conservation is tangible and everyday, which made it easier to design concrete, age-appropriate interactions. Broader climate topics would have required content that was harder to gamify.
- **Target audience decision:** We chose younger users (K-12) deliberately. Sustainability education typically targets adults; early exposure to simple habits (turn off lights, unplug devices) can shape longer-term behavior. This shaped every design and content decision downstream.
- **Managing stakeholder input:** Coordinated input from Ron Kagan (PlanetFlip lead PM), NPS science education rangers, and the SD Climate Collaborative. Each brought different priorities — game quality, scientific accuracy, and community fit. The team had to synthesize these rather than satisfy them in sequence.
- **Two-round testing loop:** Built two structured test rounds into the timeline rather than one. Round 1 tested comprehension and navigation on lo-fi wireframes; Round 2 tested full gameplay. This let us catch structural issues before committing to the hi-fi build.
- **Shipping a playable product:** The deliverable wasn't slides or a Figma — it was a working in-browser game. Scoping decisions (click-based simple interactions, three self-contained levels) kept it buildable within the course timeline.

## Outcome

Delivered a fully playable game with three levels (Bedroom, Bathroom, Kitchen) within 10 weeks. The game was presented to PlanetFlip and partner organizations. ## Reflection

Scope creep was a constant pressure — there were always more features to add. The decision to hold the line on simple click-based mechanics was the right call; it's what made the game actually shippable. I'd want to formalize that kind of scope governance earlier in the project timeline next time.
