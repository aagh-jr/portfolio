# UCSD Laundry App — UX Design

## Context

UCSD's laundry rooms are shared spaces with recurring friction: no real-time machine visibility, a persistent lost-and-found problem, and no structured way to report broken machines or issues. Students live with these problems daily. The design goal was to address them without creating a product that added complexity to an already routine task.

## My Role

Co-designer on the team. I contributed to brainstorming, participated in the mood board process, and helped design lo-fi screens and iterate toward the hi-fi prototype. [CONFIRM: which specific screens or flows I owned]

## Approach

- **Mood board to set tone:** Before any screens, the team built a mood board to land on a visual direction. The goal was "campus utility" — functional, low visual noise, trustworthy — not the kind of polished consumer aesthetic that would feel out of place for doing laundry at 11pm.
- **Spatial brainstorm first:** The team mapped the physical laundry space (bird's eye view) before designing screens. Understanding how students physically move through the room informed the information hierarchy — machine availability needs to be front and center because that's the first decision a student makes when they walk in.
- **Lost-and-found feature:** One of the non-obvious design problems that emerged from research. Designed as a photo-and-description reporting flow — simple enough to submit in under a minute, browsable by others in the building. [CONFIRM: what the final screens looked like]
- **Reporting feature:** A lightweight form flow for flagging broken machines or problems. Routed to [CONFIRM: RA/housing staff/building manager?] rather than just submitting to a void.
- **Lo-fi validation before hi-fi:** The lo-fi prototype locked down structure and flow before committing to visual decisions. This meant the hi-fi work was spent on design quality, not reworking navigation.

## Outcome

A lo-fi and hi-fi Figma prototype with at minimum three flows: machine status view, lost-and-found, and problem reporting. [CONFIRM: whether the hi-fi has a shareable link; total number of screens; visual design system used]

## Reflection

The constraint of designing for a shared community space — not a single user's personal experience — changed how I thought about every interaction. The lost-and-found feature in particular only works if enough people use it; designing for network effects on a campus app is a different problem than designing for individual utility.
